## 1. Why we're writing this

`qa automation services india` is the exact phrase an Indian CTO or engineering head Googles when manual regression has broken the release cadence, a customer-facing bug shipped on a Friday, and the board has asked why. The searcher has accepted they need outside help and is now scoring vendors — that is the bottom of the QA funnel and the highest-converting query in the cluster. The current SERP is dominated by directory pages (Clutch, GoodFirms, DesignRush) and self-serving "Top 10 QA companies in India" listicles written by the same agencies they rank. There is no honest, buyer-first checklist with real ₹ pod rates, DPDP-aware test-data clauses, and a candid disqualification sidebar — which is precisely the gap this post fills. It also serves as the conversion endpoint for the QA pillar (`/blog/qa-test-automation-2026-guide`), inheriting topical authority from the cluster and routing intent straight to `/contact`.

## 4. Questions readers want answered

1. **"How do I tell a real QA automation partner apart from a manual-QA body-shop that just renamed itself 'automation experts'?"** — The QA-washing problem. Needs concrete red flags: no live framework repo to walk through, can't name the CI runner or parallelisation strategy, demos a single Selenium IDE recording as a "case study", quotes per-tester per-month without naming the tester or the test pyramid split, can't explain flaky-test triage in plain English.
2. **"What should I actually pay a QA automation partner in India in 2026 — solo SDET, 3-person pod, full managed QA, fixed-bid project — and where is the value-for-money sweet spot?"** — Needs honest ₹ ranges: solo senior SDET retainer (₹1.5L–₹3L/month), 3-person managed pod (₹4L–₹9L/month), full managed QA function (₹10L–₹25L/month), fixed-bid framework setup + first 100 tests (₹4L–₹12L), and the trade-offs of each model over 12 months.
3. **"What goes in the contract — test-data residency, DPDP liability, IP on the framework and tests, exit/handover, staffing continuity — so we're not left with an orphaned test suite when the engagement ends?"** — Needs the clauses Indian QA buyers routinely miss: DPDP-compliant test-data masking, no production-data-in-staging clause, India-region CI runners where required, work-for-hire IP assignment on the framework + tests + CI config, named-resource continuity (or pre-approved replacement), 30-day handover with runbooks, and the exit clause that returns the repo and the CI secrets in 14 days.

## 5. Outline (H2/H3)

### H2: Before you hire anyone — the 2-question gut check
- H3: Is your codebase in a state a partner can automate against in 90 days? (deploy frequency, branch hygiene, test-environment availability)
- H3: Do you have someone in-house who can own the relationship — review PRs, approve flaky-test retirements, gate releases?
- Callout (sidebar): "Skip the partner if…" — three scenarios where the right answer is fix-CI-first, hire-one-SDET-in-house, or buy-a-tool-not-a-team, not sign-a-managed-pod.

### H2: The 12-point checklist (scoreable, one discovery call)
> Each item presented as **Ask → Green flag → Red flag**. Grouped as four pillars of three points each so the post reads as a framework, not a listicle. This section is the spine of the post; budget ~700 words here.

- H3: Pillar 1 — Proof of real automation work (points 1–3)
  - 1. Show me a live framework repo you've shipped — code, CI config, last 30 days of runs
  - 2. Walk me through your flaky-test triage process and one test you retired last month
  - 3. Name the CI runner, the parallelisation strategy, and your average pipeline duration
- H3: Pillar 2 — Fit with your stack and stage (points 4–6)
  - 4. Have you automated against our stack (web framework, mobile platform, API style)?
  - 5. Who is the named SDET on our account, and can I meet them before signing?
  - 6. What does the first 30 days deliver — audit, kill-list, first 20 stable tests?
- H3: Pillar 3 — Indian regulatory and commercial fit (points 7–9)
  - 7. How do you handle test data — masking, synthetic generation, no production data in staging?
  - 8. Where do CI runners and test artefacts live (India region for BFSI/healthcare buyers)?
  - 9. GST, TDS, milestone invoicing, named-resource contracts — is your paperwork Indian-buyer-ready?
- H3: Pillar 4 — How the engagement ends (points 10–12)
  - 10. Who owns the framework, the tests, the CI config, the test-data fixtures?
  - 11. What is the handover plan — runbooks, ownership transfer, 30-day shadow period?
  - 12. What is the exit clause — repo access, CI secrets, last invoice, all returned in 14 days?

### H2: What should this actually cost in India? (real ₹ ranges, 2026)
- H3: Solo senior SDET retainer — ₹1.5L–₹3L/month (best for: one product, < 15 engineers)
- H3: 3-person managed QA pod — ₹4L–₹9L/month (best for: 15–40 engineers, multi-surface)
- H3: Full managed QA function — ₹10L–₹25L/month (best for: 40+ engineers, regulated industry)
- H3: Fixed-bid framework setup + first 100 stable tests — ₹4L–₹12L (4–10 weeks)
- Callout (insight): A hard rule — **walk away from anyone who quotes a monthly pod rate before a paid 2-week audit.** Pricing before scoping means the risk lives with you.

### H2: The contract clauses Indian QA buyers routinely miss
- H3: DPDP-aligned test-data clause — masking, synthetic data, explicit no-production-data-in-staging
- H3: IP assignment — framework code, test cases, CI YAML, fixtures, all assigned in writing
- H3: Named-resource + replacement clause — no silent swaps, 2-week notice on changes
- H3: Sub-processor disclosure (BrowserStack, Sauce Labs, LambdaTest) and data-residency expectations
- H3: Exit clause — repo, CI secrets, documentation, all returned in 14 days

### H2: Partner, freelancer, in-house — pick the right shape
- H3: When a freelance SDET is enough (one product, < ₹2L/month budget, founder owns QA)
- H3: When a managed QA pod earns its margin (multi-surface, regulated data, you need throughput not headcount)
- H3: When you should hire in-house instead (predictable 18-month roadmap, ability to attract a senior SDET, QA is a moat)
- H3: The hybrid we recommend most often — partner pod for the first 6 months, in-house lead from month 7

## 8. Tone notes

- First-person plural "we" throughout — Toshi's voice. The "we walk every prospect through this exact checklist on the discovery call" line should ring true because it is true.
- Business-first, technical depth as proof. Each checklist point leads with the business consequence (release cadence, escaped-defect cost, audit exposure) and the technical detail (CI runner, parallelisation, flaky-test triage) lives in a 1–2 sentence aside, never the main flow.
- Honest about ourselves. This is the cluster's peak-conversion post — name our own category openly and acknowledge that we are one of the firms the reader is evaluating. The "questions we'd want you to ask us" section is the trust move competitors won't make; keep it short, specific, no humblebrag.
- Indian context as the default — ₹ figures inline (not in footnotes), DPDP and test-data residency as first-class checklist pillars, GST/TDS/named-resource paperwork mentioned in the contract section, no apology for assuming an Indian buyer.
- Willing to disqualify. The "skip the partner if…" sidebar and the "in-house instead" subsection are critical — they filter inbound to the leads we can actually serve and earn the trust of everyone else.
- Concrete > generic in every paragraph. Each of the 12 checklist points must include one red-flag sentence the reader can apply in a discovery call this week.
- No emojis, no hype ("revolutionary", "next-gen", "AI-powered everything"). Verbs: automate, instrument, parallelise, gate, retire, hand over, escrow, measure.
- Treat AI-augmented testing skeptically but fairly — acknowledge the 2026 wins (self-healing locators, generation copilots) only where they intersect the checklist (e.g., point 2 on flaky-test triage), do not let it become a sub-theme of its own.
- Close with a soft CTA — "if you want a second opinion on a QA partner you're already evaluating, book a fit call" — not a hard pitch. Buyers in this query state are allergic to closing pressure and remember the firm that didn't apply it.
