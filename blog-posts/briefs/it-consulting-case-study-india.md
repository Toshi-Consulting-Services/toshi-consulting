# Topic Brief: 3 Toshi transformations — ROI case studies (cross-pillar)

## 1. Why this topic (and why now)

The phrase "it consulting case study india" is searched by a small but unusually qualified pool: founders, COOs, and procurement heads in the final two weeks of vendor selection who want a single page that proves a firm has actually shipped — across more than one discipline, in India, with numbers attached. Pure volume is modest (sub-200/mo on the exact head, the cluster around 1.5-3K/mo combined), but the post-click behaviour is the highest-intent traffic the site receives — it is the page a CFO reads before signing the SOW.

Today's SERP is dominated by two kinds of pages, neither of which serves that reader:

1. **Global majors' case-study libraries** (IBM, Deloitte, Infosys, TCS, Accenture, Capgemini). Authoritative on brand, but written for Fortune 500 buyers — eight-figure programmes, US/EU regulators, no ₹ numbers, no SME context, no admission of what broke.
2. **Mid-tier Indian agency portfolio pages.** A grid of logos, two-line outcomes ("40% efficiency gain"), and a contact button. No narrative, no methodology, no honest lessons.

Three reasons we proceed:

1. **Cross-pillar credibility unlock.** We have shipped across all four Toshi services (AI Integration, Blockchain Consulting, QA Testing & Automations, Digital Media Marketing & Branding) but the existing case-study post (#14, AI fintech) only proves one. A three-transformation cross-pillar post is the single asset that lets a buyer evaluate Toshi as a *firm*, not as a single-service shop — and is the conversion anchor for every other pillar's pillar post.
2. **2026 timing — RBI FREE-AI, DPDP Rules 2025, and the post-XMRig security narrative.** The Indian IT-services buyer in 2026 is asking different questions than in 2024: "did you ship under DPDP?", "is the AI auditable?", "where is the data hosted?". A case-study post written *with* those overlays baked in is uncited by the global majors and unattempted by the mid-tier agencies.
3. **The conversion economics.** This is post #48 in the plan, paired with #47 (Panchkula local), #49 (pricing), and #50 (outsource vs in-house) as the four bottom-of-funnel converters. Of those four, this is the one a buyer reads *last* before requesting a call. Worth the 2,500-word investment even at low volume — payback is measured in qualified leads, not sessions.

**Verdict: proceed.** Treat as the firm's flagship credibility page, not a traffic play. Optimised for assisted conversion and for being the URL pasted into procurement evaluation matrices.

## 4. Questions a Toshi buyer would ask (FAQ entries)

1. **"Across these three transformations, what was the typical engagement size — in ₹, weeks, and team count — and how do I know my project fits?"** — Need a transparent comparison table: each case study's build cost band (₹), run cost band (₹/mo), timeline (weeks), Toshi team size (engineers + PM + governance), and the client's own internal team commitment. A "you probably fit if…" / "you probably don't fit if…" honest filter at the end.
2. **"How do you handle confidentiality and IP — what can a future client expect to see, and what stays private?"** — The post itself answers this by example (composite anonymisation, rounded numbers, named tech stacks but not client names, signed NDA + redacted screenshots). The FAQ states the policy explicitly: who signs what, what we will and won't publish, and how we handle the client's logo / quote permission.
3. **"What's the single biggest lesson that applies across all three transformations — i.e. what would I get wrong if I ran this with a less experienced firm?"** — The cross-cutting lesson section. Honest answer: the under-budgeting of integration work with legacy / on-prem systems (true for the AI study, the QA study, *and* the blockchain study), and the late wiring of observability and governance artefacts (true for all three). This is the meta-lesson — the value of hiring people who've shipped three of these, not one.

- **H2: Case Study 1 — AI process automation for a mid-sized NBFC** *(AI Integration pillar)*
  - H3: Context — who they are (anonymised NBFC composite, AUM ₹500-1,500 cr band, ~80 ops FTEs, north India HQ, 2025-26 engagement)

- **H2: Case Study 2 — Asset-tokenisation pilot for a real-estate fractional-ownership platform** *(Blockchain Consulting pillar)*
  - H3: Context — anonymised RegTech-leaning fractional-ownership startup, Mumbai HQ, pre-Series A, ~₹40 cr GMV plan for Year 1, target asset class = Grade-A commercial real estate
  - H3: Problem — needed an audit-grade on-chain ownership ledger that could survive (a) a SEBI/IFSCA conversation, (b) a DPDP audit on the KYC stack, and (c) a Big-Four diligence — and had been quoted ₹2.5 cr / 9 months by a Singapore firm
  - H3: Approach — chose a permissioned Polygon Supernet (not a public L1) for regulator-friendliness; off-chain KYC vault (India-region) with on-chain hashed attestations; smart-contract suite audited (we co-wrote the threat model); IFSCA sandbox application track in parallel with the build
  - H3: Result — production-grade pilot in 11 weeks; build ₹38L, audit ₹6L, run ~₹85K/mo; first ₹4.2 cr of tokenised AUM live by week 14; passed Big-Four IT due diligence at Series A with zero blocking findings
  - H3: Lessons — the smart-contract code was the easy part; the off-chain KYC + custodian-banking integrations consumed 60% of the timeline; we'd front-load the IFSCA sandbox conversation by 4 weeks next time

- **H2: Case Study 3 — QA automation rebuild for a Series-B B2B SaaS** *(QA Testing & Automations pillar)*
  - H3: Context — anonymised vertical-SaaS company (HR-tech adjacent), Bengaluru HQ, ~120 engineers, Series-B, deploying 2x/week with a regression suite that took 14 hours and a flaky-test rate of ~18%
  - H3: Problem — release confidence had collapsed; engineers were merging on a "looks green-ish" basis; two production incidents in Q1 traced to gaps the regression suite should have caught; QA team of 11 manual testers couldn't keep up with the product surface area
  - H3: Approach — 3-week audit of the existing suite (Selenium + a half-built Cypress migration); chose Playwright as the new spine; tiered the suite (smoke / regression / nightly); wired in Percy for visual diff and a custom flaky-test quarantine; re-trained 6 of the 11 manual testers as SDETs over 12 weeks
  - H3: Result — regression suite 14 hrs → 38 mins; flaky-test rate 18% → 2.3%; deploy frequency 2x/week → 5x/week; manual-QA headcount redeployed (not laid off — important for the story); build ₹28L over 14 weeks, run ~₹60K/mo in tooling; payback ~7 months on incident-prevention math alone
  - H3: Lessons — under-estimated the human side (the SDET retraining was the project's hardest milestone, not the framework migration); the visual-diff tool paid for itself in week 3 but we almost cut it from scope; a clear "what we don't automate" list was as important as the "what we do"
  - (Internal link out to `/blog/test-automation-roi` and `/blog/qa-test-automation-2026-guide`)

## 8. Tone notes

- First-person plural ("we", "our team", "we'd do differently", "we wouldn't take this on") throughout — this is a Toshi portfolio defended at boardroom level, not a vendor brochure.
- Narrative arc per case study is non-negotiable. Context → problem → approach → result → lessons, in that order, every time. The reader should be able to skim only the H3s and still get the story.
- Anonymise convincingly but honestly. Composite framing ("a mid-sized NBFC we worked with in 2025-26, AUM in the ₹500-1,500 cr band"), rounded numbers, named tech stacks but not client names. State the anonymisation policy up front in the "How to read this page" section — earning trust is the whole job here.
- One concrete number per H3 minimum. ₹, %, weeks, STP rate, TAT, payback months — vague paragraphs get cut. The post lives or dies on its numbers.
- Honest about what broke. The "lessons" H3 in each case study is non-negotiable and must include at least one mistake we own. The cross-cutting lessons H2 is the post's intellectual centre of gravity — if we cut anything, we don't cut that.
- Indian context is the whole point. RBI / DPDP / FREE-AI / IFSCA / NASSCOM mentioned naturally; ₹ inline; vendors named with the India-region reasoning we actually used (Azure India, AWS Mumbai, Polygon Supernet, Playwright, Percy).
- Boardroom-grade scannable layout. TL;DR + comparison table at the top, three clearly-bounded case studies in the middle, a meta-lessons + fit-filter + CTA at the end. A CFO should be able to extract the ₹ numbers in 90 seconds without scrolling text.
- Cross-pillar without being a logo wall. The three stories are AI + Blockchain + QA deliberately (skipping Digital Media is intentional — the brief is "transformation case studies", and DMM engagements are shorter / different shape; flag this choice explicitly in the framing).
- Avoid: "in today's fast-paced world", "revolutionary", "game-changing", "leveraging cutting-edge", "trusted partner", "end-to-end solutions", any sentence that could be in any vendor's brochure. No emojis. No client logo placeholders (we anonymise — that's the policy).
- Hard target: 2,400-2,600 words. TL;DR + comparison table at the top is non-negotiable for AI-overview pickup and procurement-evaluation use. FAQ block at the end is non-negotiable for FAQPage schema. The soft "fit filter" CTA earns its place by being honest about who *shouldn't* hire us.
