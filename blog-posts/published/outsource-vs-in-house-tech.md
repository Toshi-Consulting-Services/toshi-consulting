## The Build-vs-Buy Question Every Founder is Asking Wrong
The decision to outsource or build in-house tech capabilities is a critical one for startups and businesses in India. With funding compression, AI-assisted delivery, and DPDP/RBI regulations changing the landscape, the traditional approach to this question is no longer sufficient. We've seen that the right answer depends on the specific technology, team size, and project length. In this post, we'll explore the 6-factor scoring rubric we use to help our clients make informed decisions.

## The 6-Factor Scoring Rubric We Run with Every Client
Our 6-factor scoring rubric considers the following factors:
- **TCO over 3 years, fully loaded**: Calculate the total cost of ownership, including salaries, infrastructure, and management overhead.
- **Time-to-value (weeks to first shipped increment)**: Determine how quickly you can deliver a working product or feature.
- **IP sensitivity (is this your moat or your plumbing?)**: Assess the level of intellectual property sensitivity and whether it's core to your business.
- **Change cadence (does this evolve weekly, quarterly, or yearly?)**: Evaluate how frequently the technology or feature changes.
- **Talent supply in your city and salary band**: Consider the availability of skilled talent in your location and the corresponding salary range.
- **Regulatory exposure (DPDP, RBI, sectoral, cross-border)**: Assess the regulatory requirements and potential risks associated with the technology or feature.

## Applied to AI Integration — When In-House is Wrong, When Partnering is Wrong
For AI integration, the honest ₹ math shows that an in-house ML/AI engineer can cost between ₹40-90L per year, while an outsourced AI pod can cost between ₹15-45L per year. We've seen that building in-house is often the right choice when AI is the core product, while partnering is better when AI is a feature in your product. The hybrid approach, where an in-house product owner works with an outsourced AI delivery pod, is often the most effective.

## Applied to Blockchain — Specialist Depth Beats General Engineering, Always
For blockchain development, specialist depth is crucial. We've seen that in-house blockchain hires often fail due to talent supply, audit dependency, and tooling churn. Partnering with a specialist firm can provide the necessary expertise and reduce the risks associated with blockchain development.

## Applied to QA — The Most Consensus-Friendly Outsource of the Four
QA is often the most consensus-friendly outsource option. We've seen that bounded scope, mature playbooks, and AI-assisted test generation make QA a prime candidate for outsourcing. However, in-house QA wins when dealing with deeply regulated domains, security-critical code paths, or IP-sensitive test data.

## The Indian-Context Overlay Nobody Else Writes
In India, the ₹ pricing, DPDP, and RBI regulations add a unique layer of complexity to the build-vs-buy decision. We've seen that ₹ pricing by discipline, data-processing addenda, and GST/TDS considerations are essential for Indian buyers. Attrition reality, with a 12-18 month tenure pattern across AI, QA, and SDET roles, also plays a significant role in the decision.

## The Contract Clauses We Negotiate Every Time
When outsourcing, it's essential to include specific contract clauses to protect your IP, data, and interests. We've seen that work-for-hire IP assignment, data-processing addenda, sub-processor disclosure, source-code escrow, and exit clauses are critical components of any outsourcing contract.

## Three Scenarios Where the Right Answer is "Neither"
There are scenarios where the right answer is neither outsourcing nor building in-house. We've seen that if your data is too messy, it's better to fix it first before deciding. If a ₹20K/month SaaS solves the problem, buying it might be the better option. And if you have no internal owner to hold a vendor accountable, it's better to hire that person before making a decision.

To make an informed decision, we recommend a 30-day framework:

- Week 2: Get two costed proposals (one in-house hire plan, one outsourced SOW) per workstream.
