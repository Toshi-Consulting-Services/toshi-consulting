For a few years the conversation was about *whether* to move to the cloud. That question is settled. The real question now is *how* — because most enterprises are not going all-in on public cloud. They are running a deliberate hybrid mix, and the businesses that win are the ones that treat that mix as a strategy rather than an accident.

We plan and run these migrations for growing Indian businesses, and the difference between a migration that lowers cost and one that quietly raises it is almost always the planning that happens before anyone touches infrastructure.

## Why hybrid is the default, not a compromise

Few organisations have the luxury of a clean slate. There are legacy systems that are expensive to rewrite, data that has to stay in specific regions for regulatory reasons, and workloads where on-premises is genuinely cheaper at steady-state load.

Hybrid lets you put each workload where it belongs: elastic, customer-facing services on public cloud where scale is instant, and stable or regulated systems closer to home. Done right, it is not a halfway house — it is the most rational architecture for a real business with real constraints.

## Build the strategy before touching infrastructure

The most expensive migrations we see are the ones that started with a tool instead of a plan. Before any workload moves, we map the estate and assign each application one of the **6 Rs**:

- **Rehost** — lift and shift, fastest but leaves cloud savings on the table
- **Replatform** — small optimisations on the way over, often the best balance
- **Refactor** — re-architect for the cloud when the payoff justifies it
- **Repurchase** — replace with a SaaS product instead of migrating
- **Retire** — switch off what nobody actually uses
- **Retain** — leave it where it is for now and revisit later

Most estates end up with a spread across all six. The discipline of deciding per workload, with cost and risk written down, is what keeps a migration on budget.

## Cost, security, and compliance in an Indian context

Cloud bills surprise people because elastic resources are easy to start and easy to forget. We size workloads honestly, set budgets and alerts, and right-size after migration rather than over-provisioning out of caution.

On compliance, the DPDP Act, 2023 makes data location and protection a board-level concern. We classify data before it moves, keep personal and regulated data in approved regions, encrypt everything in transit and at rest, and tighten access so that moving to the cloud improves the security posture instead of spreading it thinner.

## Avoiding the common mistakes

Three patterns cause most of the pain we are called in to fix: a big-bang cutover with no rollback path, migrating an inefficient system unchanged and paying cloud prices for old waste, and treating security as a step at the end instead of a thread throughout.

The fix for all three is the same — migrate in waves, prove each pattern on a low-risk workload first, and keep observability and security in from the first move. A migration should feel boring. Boring means it is working.
