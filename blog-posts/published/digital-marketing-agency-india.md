## Before you hire anyone — a two-question gut check

Before we get to the agency checklist, two questions about your own business. If either answer is wrong, no agency in India will save you.

**Does your offer convert on cold traffic yet?** If you have warm-introduction sales, founder-network deals, and inbound from existing customers — but you've never paid for a click that converted to a meeting or a purchase — your offer hasn't been tested. Hiring an agency at this stage means paying ₹1.5L–3L/mo to find out your landing page, pricing, or positioning needs work. That's an expensive way to learn. Run a ₹50K self-managed paid pilot first, or work with a fractional growth advisor on the offer before you sign a retainer.

**Is your analytics honest enough to measure the wins?** If your GA4 is misconfigured, your CRM doesn't fire conversion events, your Meta pixel sits on three subdomains with mismatched UTM parameters, and your sales team logs deal sources in a WhatsApp group — the agency's wins are unmeasurable and the agency's losses are deniable. Spend the first two weeks fixing the analytics layer, then start the engagement on a measurable baseline.

> **Watch out:** The most expensive mistake we see in this market is signing a 6-month retainer when the actual problem is offer-market fit or broken tracking. The retainer will burn while the underlying problem stays unsolved, and you'll blame the agency for an issue that pre-dated them. Don't hire an agency yet if your offer is untested or your analytics aren't trustworthy.

## The 12-question vetting framework

We'd run any digital marketing agency india through these twelve questions in a single 45-minute discovery call. Each one is framed as **Ask → Good answer → Red flag.** Score honestly. An agency that fails three or more is a hard pass; one that fails one or two with credible reasoning may still be worth a second conversation.

### Pillar 1 — Proof the work is real

**1. Show us one case study with ROAS, CPA, and organic-sessions numbers from the last 12 months in our category.**
*Good answer:* a deck or a live screen-share showing actual platform dashboards — Google Ads spend vs. conversions, GA4 organic sessions month-on-month, Meta CPA cohorts — with the client named or at least the vertical and revenue band disclosed under NDA. *Red flag:* follower-count screenshots, "we grew their Instagram by 400%" with no revenue number, refusal to show platform-side data, or case studies dated 2022.

**2. Walk us through one campaign that underperformed — what did your attribution model show and what did you change?**
*Good answer:* a specific story — "we burnt ₹3L on a Meta prospecting campaign in month two, our MMM showed the cold audience wasn't converting, we paused, rebuilt the creative around customer-interview hooks, and CPA dropped 40% by month four." *Red flag:* "we don't really have a failure to share" or a vague answer about market conditions. Every honest agency has scars; the ones that don't are either lying or new.

**3. Who on your team will actually run our account, and can we meet them on a working call before signing?**
*Good answer:* named pod members — strategist, paid lead, SEO lead, creative — with LinkedIn profiles, and a 30-minute working session with them before contract signature. *Red flag:* you only ever meet the founder or BD lead, and "the team" never has names. That's the subcontractor pattern — the work is going to freelancers you'll never speak to.

### Pillar 2 — Fit with your business, channel mix, and scale

**4. Have you scaled paid acquisition past our current monthly media spend in our category?**
*Good answer:* a clear yes-or-no with examples. If you're a D2C brand spending ₹5L/mo on Meta, an agency whose largest account is ₹80K/mo will be learning on your budget. *Red flag:* "we can scale anything" with no proof points at your spend level.

**5. What's your point of view on channel mix for a business at our stage — and what would you *not* do?**
*Good answer:* a strong, opinionated answer — "at your ARR and CAC, we wouldn't touch programmatic display this quarter; we'd put 70% into Meta + Google search, 20% into SEO foundation, 10% into LinkedIn for the BFSI buyer segment." *Red flag:* "we'll do everything — SEO, PPC, social, content, influencer, programmatic, OTT." That's a pricing strategy, not a strategy. For the deeper view on this, our [B2B digital marketing playbook for India](/blog/b2b-digital-marketing-india) walks through how channel mix should follow buyer stage, not agency capability.

**6. What does the first 60 days look like — audit, baseline, first creative tests, first reporting cadence?**
*Good answer:* a written 60-day plan with weekly milestones — week 1–2 audit, week 3 baseline locked, week 4 first creative tests live, week 6 first reporting checkpoint, week 8 mid-engagement review. *Red flag:* "we'll start by understanding your business" with no week-by-week structure. That's billable discovery.

### Pillar 3 — Indian regulatory and commercial fit

**7. How do you handle DPDP consent on lead forms, and what does the data-processing addendum look like?**
*Good answer:* the agency operates as a data *processor* (not fiduciary), every lead form they build captures granular consent with audit logs, and the master services agreement has a data-processing addendum naming purpose-limitation and breach-notification timelines. *Red flag:* a blank stare, or "DPDP doesn't really apply to marketing." It does, and the [MeitY DPDP Act notification](https://www.meity.gov.in/data-protection-framework) carries up to ₹250 crore in penalty exposure for consent failures. The buck stops with you as the data fiduciary — but the operational failure usually happens at the agency's lead form.

**8. Are your influencer briefs ASCI-compliant, and do you have a server-side Consent Mode v2 setup for GA4?**
*Good answer:* every paid-partnership brief includes an ASCI-mandated disclosure tag (#ad, #paidpartnership, or platform-native paid-partnership labelling) per the [ASCI Influencer Advertising Guidelines](https://www.ascionline.in/the-asci-code/guidelines-influencer-advertising-in-digital-media/), and the GA4 setup uses Consent Mode v2 with server-side tagging so DPDP-consent-rejecting users don't break the conversion model. *Red flag:* the agency doesn't know what Consent Mode v2 is, or their influencer briefs leave disclosure to "the creator's discretion."

**9. How do GST input credit, 10% TDS u/s 194J, and milestone invoicing show up on your contract?**
*Good answer:* retainer invoices clearly separate the management fee (subject to 18% GST and 10% TDS u/s 194J for professional services) from pass-through media spend, milestones tied to deliverables, and a clear line-item showing the input-credit-eligible portion. *Red flag:* a single lump-sum invoice mixing management fee with media spend, no GST registration on the invoice, or pushback when you ask for milestone-based payments. We've seen finance teams spend a quarter reconciling lump-sum agency invoices — and it's avoidable.

### Pillar 4 — How the engagement ends

**10. Who owns the ad accounts, creatives, copy, keyword lists, audience lists, and tracking implementation?**
*Good answer:* you own everything. Your Google Ads account sits in your MCC (or your own login), your Meta Business Manager is yours with the agency as a user, your GA4 property is in your Google account, your server-side container is in your GCP project, and every creative asset is delivered with a work-for-hire IP assignment. *Red flag:* the agency holds ad accounts under their MCC without admin handover rights, or creatives are licensed to you instead of assigned.

**11. What's the 30-day exit plan — data export, account handover, runbook, knowledge transfer?**
*Good answer:* a contractual 30-day handover window with a documented runbook — campaign structure, naming conventions, automation rules, audience definitions, attribution setup, reporting templates. The next agency (or your in-house hire) should be able to take over without ten hours of forensic work. *Red flag:* "we'll figure that out at the end" or a punitive offboarding fee buried in the contract.

**12. What happens if a Google or Meta algorithm change wipes out the campaign you scaled — who eats the loss and how do we re-baseline?**
*Good answer:* an honest acknowledgement that algorithm risk is real, a shared-responsibility clause for re-baselining within 4–6 weeks of a documented platform change, and reference to past instances (iOS 14.5 ATT, Performance Max launches, GA4 migration) and how they navigated it. *Red flag:* "that won't happen to us." It will. Every account we run has lived through at least one platform-side reset, and the agencies worth hiring talk about it openly.

## What should this actually cost in India?

Honest ₹ bands, by service and business size. These are the numbers we'd expect to see on a 2026 retainer proposal from a competent, India-based digital marketing agency india delivering measurable work. Anything materially below the floor is either a freelancer pretending to be an agency, or a logo farm that will subcontract you out.

**SEO** — ₹25K–1L/mo for early-stage businesses in low-competition verticals (local services, niche B2B). ₹1L–3L/mo for competitive verticals — SaaS, fintech, ecommerce, BFSI. Anything under ₹25K/mo for SEO buys you template content and directory submissions, not real work. We've broken down the discipline-specific economics for one of these verticals in our [SaaS SEO India playbook](/blog/saas-seo-india).

**PPC management** — 10–20% of media spend, or a ₹15K–50K/mo floor, whichever is higher. Critical caveat: meaningful learning-phase data needs at least ₹50K–1L/mo in *actual ad spend* on top of the management fee. Below that, the algorithm doesn't have enough conversions to optimise on, and you're paying for guesswork.

**Social media management** — ₹20K–80K/mo for organic posting, community management, and reels production. Influencer fees and paid amplification sit on top, separately disclosed.

**Full-funnel retainer** (SEO + paid + social + content + analytics) — ₹1.5L–5L/mo for SMBs, ₹5L–15L+/mo for mid-market and enterprise. At the ₹5L+ band you should expect a named strategist, two channel leads, a creative resource, and an analyst — five humans minimum, not one project manager and "a team."

**Pricing model trade-offs.** Pure retainer is predictable but de-couples agency incentive from your results. Pure performance fee aligns incentive but attracts attribution arguments. The hybrid winning in 2026 is a flat retainer covering the team's billable hours plus a ROAS-trigger bonus on incremental revenue above an agreed baseline — agency gets paid for the work, and gets a bonus when they actually move the needle. For the deeper economics of how to size paid spend against the management fee, see our [performance marketing budget framework](/blog/performance-marketing-budget).

> **Key insight:** Walk away from anyone who quotes a 12-month retainer before a paid 4–6 week audit + pilot. A fixed long retainer signed before scoping pushes all the risk onto you — the agency has zero incentive to renegotiate scope when the original assumptions break, and they will break.

## The compliance layer Indian agencies still skip

This is the section most page-one results in this SERP refuse to write. We're going to write it anyway, because if you sign an agency that doesn't handle these five items, you inherit the liability.

**DPDP consent on every lead form.** Under the Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023, you're the data fiduciary and the agency is the processor. Every form they build on your behalf must capture explicit, granular, purpose-limited consent — and the consent record must be auditable. The penalty exposure is up to ₹250 crore. Make the data-processing addendum a hard contract requirement, not a "nice to have."

**ASCI disclosure on influencer campaigns.** Every paid creator partnership must carry the right disclosure label — #ad, #sponsored, or platform-native paid-partnership tagging — visible upfront in the post, not buried in a hashtag wall. The agency's brief template must build this in; if they leave it to the creator, you'll get flagged by ASCI within weeks.

**Google Consent Mode v2 + server-side GA4.** Without Consent Mode v2 implemented correctly, DPDP-consent-rejecting users break your conversion model and your modeled conversions become statistical noise. The agency should be configuring this in your Google Tag Manager server-side container — not the legacy gtag.js setup most agencies still run.

**IT Rules + intermediary obligations on ad creatives.** Creative review must screen for grievance-officer requirements on user-generated content, traceability obligations on messaging platforms, and ad-platform community-standards risk. Agencies that don't pre-screen for these get accounts suspended on Meta and Google for policy violations the buyer didn't know existed.

**GST input credit and 10% TDS u/s 194J on retainer invoices.** Agency invoices attract 18% GST (input-credit eligible if both parties are GST-registered) and you deduct 10% TDS u/s 194J on the professional-services portion of the fee. Media pass-through should be invoiced separately to avoid TDS on ad spend itself. If the agency can't structure this correctly, your finance team will spend weeks reconciling — and you'll likely lose some input credit you were entitled to.

## Agency, freelancer, in-house, or fractional — pick the right shape

Not every business needs a full-service agency. The honest answer depends on your stage and your shape.

**Solo freelancer or 2-person pod is enough when:** you're spending under ₹50K/mo on media, running one channel (usually Meta or Google search), and the founder still owns marketing strategy.

**A full-service agency earns its margin when:** you're spending past ₹2L/mo on media, running three or more channels in coordination, and need creative + analytics + ops in one stack. The integration cost of stitching three freelancers and an in-house intern together is higher than a competent agency retainer.

**Hire in-house instead when:** you have predictable always-on demand-gen, marketing is a core competency rather than a function, you have 18+ months of work visible, and you can attract a senior performance marketer (₹40L–80L+ comp band, plus stack).

**The hybrid we recommend most often:** a fractional CMO owning strategy and the buyer-side relationship, plus one specialist agency per channel owning execution. You keep the strategic muscle in-house at fractional cost; the agency owns the executional grind without owning your roadmap.
